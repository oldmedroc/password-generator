Password Security Tips

  • Use at least 12 characters for better security.
  • Include a mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols.
  • Avoid using personal information or common words.
  • Use a unique password for each account.
  • Consider using a password manager to store them safely.

History and Fun Facts About Passwords

Passwords have a fascinating history that dates back thousands of years. While we associate them with modern computing, their roots lie in ancient civilizations. For instance, in ancient Rome, soldiers used "watchwords" to identify allies during night patrols. Similarly, the Polybius square from ancient Greece was an early encryption method that could be seen as a precursor to password systems.

In the world of computing, the first digital password was invented in 1961 by Fernando Corbató at MIT. He developed it for the Compatible Time-Sharing System (CTSS) to allow multiple users to access a shared computer while keeping their files private. This simple innovation solved a practical problem but unknowingly set the stage for today's cybersecurity challenges.

By the 1970s, passwords evolved with the introduction of hashing by Robert Morris in Unix systems in 1972. Hashing encrypts passwords so they're not stored in plain text, making them harder to steal. Fast forward to the 1990s and 2000s, and passwords became ubiquitous with the rise of the internet, leading to the development of password managers and multi-factor authentication (MFA) to combat increasing cyber threats.

Funny Facts About Passwords

  • The most common password worldwide is still "123456", followed closely by "password" – despite years of warnings!
  • On average, people manage around 100 passwords in their daily lives, leading to widespread "password fatigue".
  • In the movie WarGames (1983), the password to access a military supercomputer was "Joshua", the name of the programmer's son – a classic example of a weak personal password.
  • A simple 8-character password can be cracked in under an hour by modern brute-force attacks, but adding complexity makes it take centuries.
  • In Spaceballs (1987), the air shield password is "12345" – hilariously, the same as the president's luggage combination!
  • Bill Gates predicted the death of passwords in 2004, yet here we are in 2025, still using them alongside biometrics and MFA.

Today, with advancements in AI and quantum computing, the future of passwords may involve passkeys or passwordless authentication. But for now, generating strong, unique passwords remains a key defense against hackers.
